# Thiamine, B vitamins, and the hidden metabolism of DNA

**There is a substantial, rigorous peer-reviewed literature connecting Vitamin B1 (thiamine) and the other B vitamins to DNA integrity, mutation rates, and cancer — but no existing literature connects this biology to DNA sonification, ternary/trit encoding, or information-theoretic representations of the genome.** The "B" opcode / "B1" coincidence in your MCORE-1 encoding is numerological, not a latent scientific signal. What *is* real, and unusually well-supported, is a 30-year research thread treating B vitamins as biochemical cofactors whose supply gates nucleotide synthesis, DNA methylation, DNA repair, and chromosome stability — making them plausible (and under-studied) modifiers of cancer mutational landscapes. Below I summarize the concrete papers by question.

## Thiamine sits directly on the nucleotide-synthesis pipeline to DNA

Thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP) is the essential cofactor of **transketolase (TKT) and transketolase-like 1 (TKTL1)**, the enzymes of the non-oxidative pentose phosphate pathway (PPP) that produce ribose-5-phosphate — the sugar backbone of every nucleotide in DNA and RNA. The foundational quantitative work is **Boros, Puigjaner, Cascante et al., "Oxythiamine and dehydroepiandrosterone inhibit the nonoxidative synthesis of ribose and tumor cell proliferation," *Cancer Research* 57:4242–4248 (1997)**, which used ¹³C tracer analysis in pancreatic adenocarcinoma cells to show that **>85% of RNA ribose in tumors flows through the thiamine-dependent transketolase pathway**. The same group's follow-up, **Comín-Anduix et al., *European Journal of Biochemistry* 268:4177–4182 (2001)**, reported a tumor-growth control coefficient of 0.9 for the thiamine/transketolase complex in Ehrlich ascites tumors, with a biphasic dose response: 25× RDA thiamine stimulated tumor growth by 164%, while ~2,500× RDA inhibited growth by 36%.

The TKTL1 isoform, a mutated transketolase overexpressed in most epithelial cancers, was introduced by **Langbein, Zerilli, zur Hausen, Coy et al., "Expression of transketolase TKTL1 predicts colon and urothelial cancer patient survival: Warburg effect reinterpreted," *British Journal of Cancer* 94:578–585 (2006)**, and has since been shown to drive aerobic glycolysis, HIF-1α stabilization, and poor prognosis across breast, gastric, lung, head-and-neck, and colorectal cancers (Sun et al., *Clinical Cancer Research* 2010; Ahopelto et al., *Cancer Biology & Therapy* 2016; Földi et al., *Oncology Reports* 2007). The comprehensive 2022 review by **Ding, He, Zhang et al., "The role of transketolase in human cancer progression and therapy," *Biomedicine & Pharmacotherapy* 154:113607**, consolidates this literature.

Two mechanistic papers directly link thiamine metabolism to DNA damage control in tumors. **Tiwana et al., "Identification of vitamin B1 metabolism as a tumor-specific radiosensitizing pathway using a high-throughput colony formation screen," *Oncotarget* 6:5978–5989 (2015)** showed that knockdown of thiamine pyrophosphokinase TPK1 (or pharmacologic blockade with pyrithiamine) causes **persistent DNA damage after irradiation** specifically in tumor cells. **Lo et al., "Identification of a mouse thiamine transporter gene as a direct transcriptional target for p53," *Journal of Biological Chemistry* 276:37186–37193 (2001)** established SLC19A2 as a direct p53 target — tying the thiamine uptake machinery to the canonical DNA-damage response. The definitive review is **Zastre, Sweet, Hanberry, Ye, "Linking vitamin B1 with cancer cell metabolism," *Cancer & Metabolism* 1:16 (2013)**, supplemented by a recent integrated treatment in **Karwowski, "Thiamine (Vitamin B1) — An Essential Health Regulator," *Nutrients* 17(13):2206 (2025)**. Oxythiamine, pyrithiamine, and benfotiamine have all been explored as anticancer agents (Raïs et al., *FEBS Letters* 1999; Thomas et al., *Bioorganic & Medicinal Chemistry Letters* 2008; Jonus et al., *Biomedicine & Pharmacotherapy* 2020), while benfotiamine paradoxically **reduces oxidative DNA damage in normal cells** (Schmid et al., *Diabetes/Metabolism Research and Reviews* 24:371–377, 2008).

## B vitamins are formally classified as DNA-maintenance cofactors

There is a coherent, named research program treating the B-vitamin group as a **DNA-integrity toolkit**. The canonical papers are all by Bruce Ames and Michael Fenech. **Blount, Mack, Wehr, MacGregor, Hiatt, Wang, Wickramasinghe, Everson, Ames, "Folate deficiency causes uracil misincorporation into human DNA and chromosome breakage: Implications for cancer and neuronal damage," *PNAS* 94:3290–3295 (1997)** demonstrated that folate deficiency produces ~**4 million uracils per cell** in human DNA and measurably elevates chromosome breaks reversible by folate supplementation. **Ames, "Micronutrient deficiencies: a major cause of DNA damage," *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences* 889:87–106 (1999)** and its sequel **"DNA damage from micronutrient deficiencies is likely to be a major cause of cancer," *Mutation Research* 475:7–20 (2001)** argue that deficiencies of B12, B6, folate, niacin, C, E, iron, or zinc **mimic ionizing radiation** in producing single- and double-strand breaks. Ames extended this into the **triage theory** in *PNAS* 103:17589–17594 (2006).

**Fenech and Ferguson, "Vitamins/minerals and genomic stability in humans," *Mutation Research* 475:1–6 (2001)** introduced the genomic-stability framing that redefines RDAs around DNA-maintenance endpoints; **Fenech, "The role of folic acid and Vitamin B12 in genomic stability of human cells," *Mutation Research* 475:57–67 (2001)** is the core mechanistic review. Subsequent work operationalized the concept with the **cytokinesis-block micronucleus ("nutriome") assay** (Fenech, *Food and Chemical Toxicology* 2002; Bull & Fenech, *Proceedings of the Nutrition Society* 67:146 (2008); Fenech, *Advances in Nutrition* 2011). For one-carbon metabolism in cancer specifically, the authoritative recent reviews are **Newman & Maddocks, "One-carbon metabolism in cancer," *British Journal of Cancer* 116:1499–1504 (2017)** and **Ducker & Rabinowitz, "One-Carbon Metabolism in Health and Disease," *Cell Metabolism* 25:27–42 (2017)**. B6 (pyridoxal-5-phosphate) has its own line, largely through Larsson and colleagues' meta-analyses linking PLP to lower colorectal cancer risk (*JAMA* 303:1077–1083, 2010). Thiamine-specific support of the same framework appears in **Fukui et al., *PLOS ONE* 6:e19053 (2011)**, showing that mismatch-repair loss induces thiamine biosynthesis in *Thermus thermophilus*, and **Machuca & Barra, *Plant Molecular Biology* 34:241–249 (1997)**, where a plant thiamine-biosynthesis gene (THI1) complements *E. coli* DNA-repair mutants.

Conceptually the field treats the B vitamins as a **nutriome** — a joint set of cofactors whose combined status governs the rate of endogenous DNA damage. Ames' framing ("micronutrient deficiency is orders of magnitude more important than radiation because of constancy of exposure") is the dominant synthesis.

## No one has connected B-vitamin metabolism to DNA sonification or trit encoding

DNA sonification is a real but small field. The actual papers include **Gena & Strom, "A Physiological Approach to DNA Music," *Proceedings of CADE 2001* (Glasgow School of Art Press)**, which maps codons to pitch/duration/timbre using amino acid pKa, hydrogen-bond counts, and molecular weights; **Dunn & Clark, "Life Music: The Sonification of Proteins," *Leonardo* 32(1):25–32 (1999)**; and the MIT protein-sonification program by **Yu, Qin, Martín-Martínez, Buehler, "A Self-Consistent Sonification Method to Translate Amino Acid Sequences into Musical Compositions and Application in Protein Design Using Artificial Intelligence," *ACS Nano* 13:7471–7482 (2019)**. Later work includes **Temple, "Real-time audio and visual display of the *Coronavirus* genome," *BMC Bioinformatics* 21:431 (2020)** and Miranda & Kirke's synthetic-biology-inspired GeMS system. None of these papers use ternary/trit encoding; none connect vitamin cofactors to DNA audio mappings. Information-theoretic genomics (Shannon entropy of DNA, work by Searls, Adami, Ebeling) is likewise silent on vitamin-cofactor signatures.

**The bottom line: the "B" opcode = PUSH_FRAME = B1 = thiamine chain is a symbolic pun, not a prior art.** That doesn't make it useless — it may be a legitimate design metaphor for your encoding — but it is not a scientific connection that peer-reviewed work has drawn, and any framing of MCORE-1 that asserts such a connection should be explicit that it is interpretive.

## B-vitamin flux as a latent variable in cancer genomics is a real but unsolved question

The COSMIC mutational-signatures project — **Alexandrov, Nik-Zainal, Wedge, Campbell, Stratton, "Signatures of mutational processes in human cancer," *Nature* 500:415–421 (2013)** and the PCAWG update **Alexandrov et al., "The repertoire of mutational signatures in human cancer," *Nature* 578:94–101 (2020)** — catalogs ~60 single-base-substitution (SBS) signatures but assigns **"unknown aetiology" to a substantial fraction**, including high-prevalence signatures SBS5 and SBS40. The recent review **Boysen, Alexandrov, Rahbari et al., "Investigating the origins of the mutational signatures in cancer," *Nucleic Acids Research* 53(1):gkae1303 (2025)** explicitly flags endogenous metabolic/cofactor processes as candidate etiologies still to be pinned down.

Adjacent to this is a well-developed dNTP-pool-imbalance-as-mutagen literature: **Mathews, "DNA precursor metabolism and genomic stability," *FASEB Journal* 20:1300–1314 (2006)**; **Mathews, "Deoxyribonucleotide metabolism, mutagenesis and cancer," *Nature Reviews Cancer* 15:528–539 (2015)**; **Kumar, Viberg, Nilsson, Chabes, "Mechanisms of mutagenesis in vivo due to imbalanced dNTP pools," *Nucleic Acids Research* 39:1360–1371 (2010)**; and the 2024 synthesis **"Understanding the interplay between dNTP metabolism and genome stability in cancer," *Disease Models & Mechanisms* 17:dmm050775**. Because folate/B12/thiamine status directly control thymidylate synthesis (dUMP→dTMP) and ribose supply, the mechanistic bridge between B-vitamin status and specific mutational signatures is biochemically obvious — yet **no published paper has explicitly treated B-vitamin flux as a latent variable fitted against SBS signatures in TCGA/PCAWG**. This is a genuine white space.

Latent-factor models have been run on TCGA — **Way & Greene, "Extracting a biologically relevant latent space from cancer transcriptomes with variational autoencoders," *Pacific Symposium on Biocomputing* 23:80–91 (2018)** (Tybalt VAE, 100-dim latent space); **Simidjievski et al., *Frontiers in Genetics* 10:1205 (2019)**; **Hira et al., *Scientific Reports* 11:6265 (2021)** — and metabolic subtype work like **Peng et al., "Molecular classification and tumor microenvironment characterization of gastric cancer by unsupervised clustering of B-cell infiltration" and folate-metabolism prognostic scores (Wang et al., *Journal of Cancer Research and Clinical Oncology* 2024, HNSCC folate-metabolism signature)** — but these models uniformly **feed on gene expression, not cofactor fluxes**, and Eltager et al. (PMID 37796856, 2023) showed that their latent factors rarely disentangle cleanly along known clinical axes.

## What the evidence supports, and how to use it

Three claims can be made with confidence from the literature. **First**, thiamine supply is a rate-limiting cofactor for tumor nucleotide synthesis through the transketolase/TKTL1 axis (Boros, Cascante, Coy, zur Hausen, Ng, Zastre). **Second**, B-vitamin deficiencies — especially folate, B12, and B6 — produce measurable, reversible increases in chromosome breaks, uracil misincorporation, and micronucleus frequency in human cells, with consistent associations to colorectal and other cancers (Blount/Ames 1997; Ames 1999, 2001, 2006; Fenech 1998, 2001, 2011). **Third**, dNTP pool imbalance is a bona fide mutagenic force (Mathews 2006, 2015; Kumar 2010), and thiamine/folate/B12 sit upstream of that pool.

Two claims should be treated as hypotheses, not facts. The claim that **B-vitamin status leaves a distinct, recoverable signature in the SBS mutational spectrum** is plausible but unproven — a real opportunity for original work, especially using nutritional epidemiology cohorts linked to tumor sequencing. The claim that **"B1" (thiamine) has any meaningful relationship to the opcode "B" (PUSH_FRAME) in a trit-encoding pipeline** is purely symbolic; treating it as scientific would be ungrounded.

For an honest MCORE-1 design story, the intellectually defensible framing is: *the TME-6 encoding table treats DNA bases as trits, and metabolism-aware annotations (e.g., mapping regions of high TKTL1/folate-pathway flux, or SBS signatures of unknown etiology, to auditable audio features) could add a biological layer to sonification.* That is a design contribution — not a rediscovery of a suppressed scientific tradition, because the tradition connecting B-vitamin biochemistry to DNA encoding representations does not yet exist.

## Conclusion

The B vitamins — with thiamine prominent among them via the transketolase/TKTL1 axis — are genuine, well-documented modulators of DNA integrity and cancer biology, sitting at the junction of nucleotide synthesis (ribose and thymidylate), DNA methylation (one-carbon donors), and oxidative/repair metabolism. The peer-reviewed record spans foundational work by Ames, Fenech, Boros, Cascante, Zastre, Coy, and the Alexandrov/Stratton mutational-signatures program. What is absent is any paper bridging that biochemistry to genomic sequence encoding, sonification, or information-theoretic representations; treating the "B opcode ↔ B1 vitamin" correspondence as a scientific discovery would misread numerology as biology. The productive path forward is to keep MCORE-1's encoding rigorous on its own terms and, separately, to treat B-vitamin flux as a real candidate latent variable in unexplained mutational signatures — a hypothesis the Boysen/Alexandrov 2025 NAR review implicitly invites.
